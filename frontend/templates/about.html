About the Project

Advanced AI system for NASA's Exoplanet Detection Challenge

Purpose & Summary

Exoplanet Hunter AI is an advanced machine learning web application designed to assist astronomers and researchers in classifying exoplanet candidates from NASA's Kepler mission data.

This project was developed for the NASA Space Apps Challenge 2025 to demonstrate how cutting-edge artificial intelligence—combining deep learning, ensemble methods, and Constitutional AI principles—can enhance scientific discovery with improved accuracy, transparency, and reliability.

The system classifies exoplanet candidates into three categories: CONFIRMED (verified exoplanets), CANDIDATE (potential exoplanets requiring further observation), and FALSE POSITIVE (non-planetary signals). Users input stellar and planetary features to receive instant classifications with confidence scores, uncertainty estimates, and AI-generated explanations.

Why This Site Exists: Identifying real exoplanets among thousands of potential candidates is challenging and time-consuming. This AI-powered tool helps researchers quickly and accurately classify candidates, prioritize which objects need follow-up observation, and understand the reasoning behind each prediction—accelerating the pace of exoplanet discovery.

Project Overview

Exoplanet Hunter AI combines state-of-the-art machine learning architectures to provide accurate, explainable classifications of exoplanet candidates from the Kepler space telescope mission.

Built for NASA Space Apps Challenge 2025, this system integrates a CNN-Transformer hybrid model with an ensemble of gradient boosting methods (LightGBM and XGBoost), incorporating Constitutional AI principles to ensure scientific integrity, uncertainty quantification, and transparent decision-making.

The Challenge

NASA's Kepler mission identified thousands of potential exoplanet candidates through transit photometry—measuring tiny dips in stellar brightness as planets pass in front of their host stars. However, many signals can mimic planetary transits:

  • Binary star systems creating false signals
  • Background eclipsing binaries
  • Instrumental artifacts and noise
  • Stellar activity and variability

Manually verifying each candidate is resource-intensive and requires extensive follow-up observations. Our AI system automates this classification process while providing uncertainty estimates and flagging cases that require human expert review.

Methodology

Our Approach
  1. Data Processing: Comprehensive cleaning, missing value imputation, feature scaling, and SMOTE resampling to handle class imbalance in the Kepler Cumulative Dataset
  2. CNN-Transformer Architecture: Hybrid deep learning model with convolutional layers for local pattern extraction, positional encoding, and multi-head self-attention mechanisms for capturing complex dependencies
  3. Ensemble Learning: Soft voting ensemble combining LightGBM and XGBoost predictions with the CNN-Transformer output for robust, stable classifications
  4. Uncertainty Quantification: Monte Carlo Dropout with 100 forward passes provides epistemic and aleatoric uncertainty measures, enabling the model to express confidence levels
  5. Constitutional AI: Built-in scientific integrity principles with automatic review flagging, transparent explanations, and safety constraints prioritizing accurate discovery
  6. Validation: Rigorous evaluation on held-out test data with comprehensive metrics for each class, especially focusing on the challenging CANDIDATE category

Key Features Analyzed

The system analyzes 12 critical stellar and planetary features from the Kepler dataset:

  • KOI Score: Disposition score (0-1)
  • Transit Depth: Signal depth in parts per million
  • Orbital Period: Days per orbit
  • Transit Duration: Hours of transit event
  • Stellar Temperature: Effective temperature in Kelvin
  • Stellar Radius: In solar radii
  • Planet Radius: In Earth radii
  • Equilibrium Temperature: Planetary temperature
  • Stellar Mass: In solar masses
  • Surface Gravity: log10(cm/s²)
  • Insolation Flux: Relative to Earth
  • Signal-to-Noise Ratio: Model SNR
